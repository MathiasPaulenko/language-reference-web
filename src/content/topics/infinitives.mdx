## Overview

The infinitive is the default, uninflected form of a verb — the shape you would find in a dictionary. Despite its apparent simplicity, the infinitive carries different grammatical privileges in each of the three languages.

Spanish treats the infinitive as a fully fledged noun: it can serve as subject, object, or predicate without any special marking. English splits its infinitives into two camps — the to-infinitive and the bare infinitive — with the choice governed by the preceding verb or modal. Chinese does not even have a category called "infinitive," because its verbs never inflect in the first place; the base form is the only form.

These differences create significant transfer errors. Spanish speakers learning English may drop "to" where it is required; English speakers learning Spanish may overuse it; and speakers of both may search in vain for a Chinese infinitive marker that does not exist.

## Explanation

### Spanish

Spanish infinitives end in **-ar**, **-er**, or **-ir**. They are true verbal nouns:

- **As subject**: *Nadar es saludable* (Swimming is healthy).
- **As object**: *Quiero comer* (I want to eat).
- **After prepositions**: *antes de salir* (before leaving), *para estudiar* (in order to study).
- **After perception verbs**: *Vi caer la hoja* (I saw the leaf fall).

Spanish infinitives can even take direct objects and adverbs, just like finite verbs: *Quiero leer este libro mañana* (I want to read this book tomorrow).

### English

English infinitives come in two forms:

- **To-infinitive**: used after most verbs (*want to go*), as subjects (*To err is human*), and in purpose clauses (*I came here to learn*).
- **Bare infinitive**: used after modals (*can go*, *must see*), after *let* and *make* (*let him go*), and after perception verbs in active voice (*I saw him leave*).

The split is largely lexical: you must memorize which verbs take which form. Some verbs accept both with a meaning change: *I stopped to smoke* (purpose) vs *I stopped smoking* (habit).

### Chinese

Chinese verbs have no finite/non-finite distinction. The same form appears in every context:

- **Subject position**: <ruby>游<rt>yóu</rt></ruby><ruby>泳<rt>yǒng</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>健<rt>jiàn</rt></ruby><ruby>康<rt>kāng</rt></ruby> (Swimming is healthy) — no special marking.
- **After a modal**: <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>会<rt>huì</rt></ruby><ruby>游<rt>yóu</rt></ruby><ruby>泳<rt>yǒng</rt></ruby> (I can swim) — the verb follows the modal directly.
- **After another verb**: <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>想<rt>xiǎng</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby><ruby>饭<rt>fàn</rt></ruby> (I want to eat) — the second verb is simply concatenated.
- **After prepositions**: <ruby>为<rt>wéi</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby><ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby><ruby>习<rt>xí</rt></ruby> (in order to study) — the verb follows <ruby>为<rt>wéi</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby> directly.

Because there is no inflection, there is no need for an infinitive marker. The concept of "dictionary form" is identical to the concept of "verb."

## Key Takeaways

- Spanish infinitives end in -ar, -er, -ir and can function as nouns freely.
- English has to-infinitives and bare infinitives (after modals); the choice is lexically determined.
- Chinese has no infinitive form because verbs are already unmarked in their base state.
- Not all verbs that take infinitives in Spanish take infinitives in English, and vice versa.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Adding 'to' after Spanish modal verbs: 'Puedo to nadar'

   → Spanish modals are followed directly by the infinitive: *Puedo nadar* (no 'to').

   English modals (can, must, will) also drop 'to', but most other verbs require it: *I want to swim*.

2. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Using the Spanish infinitive where English needs a gerund: 'I enjoy to swim'

   → English 'enjoy' takes a gerund: *I enjoy swimming*. Spanish 'disfrutar' also takes the infinitive: *Disfruto nadar*.

   Verb complementation patterns differ across languages and must be learned individually.

3. **Chinese speakers learning Spanish**: Translating English 'to' as Chinese <ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby> before every verb

   → Chinese never marks infinitives. Use the bare verb: <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>想<rt>xiǎng</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby> (I want to eat), not <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>想<rt>xiǎng</rt></ruby><ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby>.

   <ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby> can mean 'go' and is only used when motion is actually intended.
