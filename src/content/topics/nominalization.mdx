## Overview

Nominalization turns non-nouns (verbs, adjectives, phrases) into noun-like entities that can function as subjects or objects.

- **Spanish**: Uses *lo + adjective* for abstract quality, *el/la + adjective* for people, and infinitives freely as subjects. Relative clauses with *que* nominalize.
- **English**: Uses *the + adjective* for classes of people, *-ing* forms as gerunds, infinitives as subjects, and *that/wh-* clauses as noun clauses.
- **Chinese**: Uses *<ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>* as a universal nominalizer. Any modifier + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> can stand as a noun phrase. No articles needed.

## Spanish

### Lo + adjective (abstract nominalization)

*Lo* + adjective creates an abstract noun:

- *Lo bueno es que...* (The good thing is that...)
- *No entiendo lo difícil.* (I don't understand the difficult part.)
- *Dime lo importante.* (Tell me the important thing.)
- *Lo mismo me dijo ella.* (She told me the same thing.)
- *Lo peor es que no vino.* (The worst thing is that he didn't come.)

### El/la + adjective (people)

- *Los ricos no pagan impuestos.* (The rich don't pay taxes.)
- *Ayudemos a los pobres.* (Let's help the poor.)
- *Los jóvenes de hoy...* (Young people today...)

### Infinitive as subject

Spanish freely uses infinitives as subjects:

- *Nadar es saludable.* (Swimming is healthy.)
- *Fumar está prohibido.* (Smoking is prohibited.)
- *Estudiar requiere dedicación.* (Studying requires dedication.)

### Infinitive as object

- *Me gusta cantar.* (I like singing.)
- *Decidió irse.* (He decided to leave.)
- *Intenta entender.* (Try to understand.)

### Relative clause nominalization

- *El que vino fue Juan.* (The one who came was Juan.)
- *Lo que dijo es verdad.* (What he said is true.)
- *Cualquiera que venga será bienvenido.* (Whoever comes will be welcome.)

### Noun clauses with que

- *Es importante que estudies.* (It is important that you study.)
- *Me alegra que hayas venido.* (I'm glad you came.)

## English

### The + adjective

Refers to a class of people:

- *The rich* (rich people)
- *The poor* (poor people)
- *The elderly* (elderly people)
- *The good, the bad, and the ugly*

**Note**: Not all adjectives work this way:

- ✅ *the good, the bad, the ugly*
- ❌ *the happy* → *happy people*
- ❌ *the tall* → *tall people*

### Gerund (-ing) as subject

English prefers gerunds as subjects:

- *Swimming is fun.*
- *Smoking is bad for you.*
- *Learning languages takes time.*

### Infinitive as subject (less common)

- *To swim is fun.* (formal/archaic)
- *To err is human.* (fixed expression)
- *To be or not to be...* (Shakespeare)

### Infinitive as object

- *I want to swim.*
- *She decided to leave.*
- *He tried to understand.*

### Noun clauses

| Introducer | Example |
|---|---|
| that | *I know **that** he is right.* |
| wh- | *I don't know **what** to do.* |
| whether/if | *I wonder **whether** he will come.* |
| wh- + to | *I don't know **how** to swim.* |

### Possessive + gerund

- *I don't like **his singing**.* (the fact that he sings)
- *Do you mind **my asking**?* (the fact that I ask)

### Free relative clauses

- *What you need is rest.*
- *Whoever comes will be welcome.*
- *I'll take whatever you have.*

## Chinese

### <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> as universal nominalizer

<ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> turns any modifier into a noun phrase:

**Adjective + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>**:

- <ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (the good one / good things)
- <ruby>贵<rt>guì</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (the expensive one)
- <ruby>红<rt>hóng</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (the red one)

**Verb phrase + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>**:

- <ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> (the person who came)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>买<rt>mǎi</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (what I bought)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (what he said)

**Pronoun + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>** (possessive):

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (mine)
- <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (yours)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (ours)

### Verb as subject

Chinese verbs can be subjects directly:

- <ruby>游<rt>yóu</rt></ruby><ruby>泳<rt>yǒng</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>。(Swimming is good.)
- <ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby><ruby>习<rt>xí</rt></ruby><ruby>需<rt>xū</rt></ruby><ruby>要<rt>yào</rt></ruby><ruby>时<rt>shí</rt></ruby><ruby>间<rt>jiān</rt></ruby>。(Studying requires time.)

No special marking needed.

### <ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby> + verb + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (formal nominalization)

<ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby> makes a verb more noun-like (formal/written):

- <ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>见<rt>jiàn</rt></ruby> (what is seen)
- <ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>闻<rt>wén</rt></ruby> (what is heard)
- <ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>作<rt>zuò</rt></ruby><ruby>所<rt>suǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>为<rt>wéi</rt></ruby> (actions and behavior)

### Verb + <ruby>性<rt>xìng</rt></ruby> / <ruby>度<rt>dù</rt></ruby> / <ruby>率<rt>shuài</rt></ruby> (abstract noun suffixes)

| Suffix | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| <ruby>性<rt>xìng</rt></ruby> | -ness / -ity | <ruby>重<rt>zhòng</rt></ruby><ruby>要<rt>yào</rt></ruby><ruby>性<rt>xìng</rt></ruby> (importance) |
| <ruby>度<rt>dù</rt></ruby> | -degree | <ruby>难<rt>nán</rt></ruby><ruby>度<rt>dù</rt></ruby> (difficulty) |
| <ruby>率<rt>lǜ</rt></ruby> | -rate | <ruby>成<rt>chéng</rt></ruby><ruby>功<rt>gōng</rt></ruby><ruby>率<rt>lǜ</rt></ruby> (success rate) |
| <ruby>者<rt>zhě</rt></ruby> | -er / person | <ruby>读<rt>dú</rt></ruby><ruby>者<rt>zhě</rt></ruby> (reader) |
| <ruby>员<rt>yuán</rt></ruby> | -member | <ruby>会<rt>huì</rt></ruby><ruby>员<rt>yuán</rt></ruby> (member) |

### Contextual head noun

In speech, Chinese often omits the head noun after <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>, leaving it to context:

- A: <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>要<rt>yào</rt></ruby><ruby>哪<rt>nǎ</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>ge</rt></ruby>？(Which one do you want?)
- B: <ruby>红<rt>hóng</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>。(The red one.)

## Comparison at a glance

| Pattern | Spanish | English | Chinese |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Abstract quality | lo + adj | the + adj + thing / what is + adj | adj + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> |
| Class of people | los/las + adj | the + adj | adj + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> + <ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> |
| Verb as subject | Infinitive | Gerund (-ing) / infinitive | Verb (unchanged) |
| Verb as object | Infinitive | Gerund / infinitive | Verb (unchanged) |
| Relative nominalization | el que / lo que | the one who / what | verb + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> |
| Possessive nominalization | el mío / lo mío | mine | pronoun + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> |
| Formal abstraction | - | -ness / -ity | verb + <ruby>性<rt>xìng</rt></ruby> / <ruby>度<rt>dù</rt></ruby> / <ruby>率<rt>shuài</rt></ruby> |

## Common mistakes

1. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Using gerund as subject: *Nadando es bueno* → *Nadar es bueno*

2. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Using infinitive as subject: *To swim is good* → *Swimming is good* (more natural)

3. **English speakers learning Chinese**: Omitting <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>: *<ruby>红<rt>hóng</rt></ruby>* alone is not a noun phrase → *<ruby>红<rt>hóng</rt></ruby>**<ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>*** is

4. **Chinese speakers learning English**: Using the + adjective for everything: *the happy* → *happy people*
