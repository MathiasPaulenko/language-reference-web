## Overview

Grammatical mood is the grammaticalization of the speaker's attitude toward the proposition. It tells the listener not what happened, but how the speaker feels about what happened — whether it is a fact, a wish, a command, or a hypothetical scenario.

Spanish retains one of the most robust mood systems in modern Indo-European languages, with fully conjugated subjunctive and conditional paradigms. English has largely dismantled its mood system: the subjunctive survives in only a few fossilized constructions, and the conditional is expressed analytically with *would*. Chinese never developed grammatical mood at all; instead, speaker attitude floats free of the verb, carried by sentence-final particles, adverbs, and discourse context.

For multilingual learners, mood is a collision zone. Spanish speakers must accept that English rarely marks mood on the verb. English and Chinese speakers must internalize that Spanish requires them to choose a different conjugation not because of time or subject, but because of doubt, desire, or emotion.

## Explanation

### Spanish

Spanish distinguishes four moods, each with its own set of conjugations:

- **Indicative**: states facts and certainty. *Ella habla español* (She speaks Spanish).
- **Subjunctive**: expresses doubt, desire, emotion, hypothesis, and negation. Triggered by specific conjunctions and matrix verbs: *Quiero que *vengas**, *Es posible que *llueva**.
- **Imperative**: direct commands. Positive commands use special forms (*Ven*, *Dime*); negative commands use the present subjunctive (*No vengas*, *No digas*).
- **Conditional**: hypothetical outcomes and polite requests. *Compraría un coche* (I would buy a car); *¿Podrías ayudarme?* (Could you help me?).

The subjunctive appears in two tenses (present and imperfect) and two aspects (perfect and pluperfect), creating a fully parallel system to the indicative.

### English

English mood is minimal:

- **Indicative**: the default for all statements. *She speaks Spanish*.
- **Subjunctive**: survives in three narrow pockets:
  - Mandative: *It is essential that he **be** present*.
  - Formulaic: *Long **live** the king*; *God **bless** you*.
  - Counterfactual: *If I **were** you*.
- **Imperative**: identical to the base form. *Come here*.
- **Conditional**: not a mood but a construction — *would* + infinitive. *I would travel*.

For most everyday communication, English speakers express attitude through auxiliaries (*might*, *could*, *should*) and adverbs (*probably*, *hopefully*) rather than verb form changes.

### Chinese

Chinese has no grammatical category of mood. The verb never changes to indicate attitude. Instead:

- **Indicative / neutral**: no marker. <ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby><ruby>医<rt>yī</rt></ruby><ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby> (She studies medicine).
- **Wish / desire**: use verbs like <ruby>希<rt>xī</rt></ruby><ruby>望<rt>wàng</rt></ruby> (hope), <ruby>想<rt>xiǎng</rt></ruby> (want). <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>希<rt>xī</rt></ruby><ruby>望<rt>wàng</rt></ruby><ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby> (I hope you come).
- **Hypothetical**: use <ruby>如<rt>rú</rt></ruby><ruby>果<rt>guǒ</rt></ruby> (if) + <ruby>就<rt>jiù</rt></ruby> (then). <ruby>如<rt>rú</rt></ruby><ruby>果<rt>guǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>下<rt>xià</rt></ruby><ruby>雨<rt>yǔ</rt></ruby>，<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>就<rt>jiù</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby> (If it rains, I won't go).
- **Command**: tone and context suffice; <ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby> softens: <ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby> (Come). <ruby>过<rt>guò</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby>！ (Come here! — forceful).
- **Doubt**: adverbs like <ruby>可<rt>kě</rt></ruby><ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby> (possibly), <ruby>也<rt>yě</rt></ruby><ruby>许<rt>xǔ</rt></ruby> (maybe). <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>可<rt>kě</rt></ruby><ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby><ruby>假<rt>jiǎ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (This might be false).

Sentence-final particles add nuance: <ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby> turns a statement into a question; <ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby> makes a suggestion; <ruby>呢<rt>ne</rt></ruby> invites reciprocity; <ruby>啊<rt>ā</rt></ruby> softens or emphasizes. These particles are the closest Chinese comes to encoding speaker attitude grammatically.
