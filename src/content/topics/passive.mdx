## Overview

The passive voice shifts focus from the doer to the receiver of an action.

- **Spanish**: The passive is relatively rare. Spanish speakers prefer the active voice or the impersonal *se* construction. When used, the past participle agrees in gender and number with the subject.
- **English**: The passive is extremely common — in formal writing, scientific texts, and when the agent is unknown or unimportant.
- **Chinese**: Uses <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> to mark the passive. Historically carried a negative or adversative tone (something bad happened to the subject), though modern usage is broadening.

## Spanish

### True passive (ser + past participle)

Formed with *ser* + past participle. The participle agrees in gender and number:

- *La carta **fue escrita** por María.* (The letter was written by María.)
- *Las casas **fueron vendidas**.* (The houses were sold.)
- *El libro **será publicado** el mes que viene.* (The book will be published next month.)

**Important**: The participle is an adjective here — it must agree.

### Stative passive (estar + past participle)

*Estar* + participle describes a **state or result**, not an action:

- *La puerta **está cerrada**.* (The door is closed. — state)
- *La ventana **está rota**.* (The window is broken. — result)

**Distinction**:

- *La puerta **fue cerrada** por Juan.* (passive action — Juan closed it)
- *La puerta **está cerrada**.* (stative result — it's in a closed state)

### Impersonal se (preferred over passive)

Spanish usually avoids the passive. Instead, use *se* + 3rd person verb:

- *Se **venden** casas.* (Houses for sale. / Houses are sold.)
- *Se **habla** español.* (Spanish spoken here.)
- *Se **dice** que...* (It is said that...)

This is the most natural way to express passive meaning in Spanish.

### Reflexive passive

Some verbs use a reflexive form with passive meaning:

- *El vino **se vende** bien.* (The wine sells well.)
- *La fruta **se come** fresca.* (The fruit is eaten fresh.)

## English

### Be-passive (standard)

Formed with *be* + past participle. The agent is optional:

- *The letter **was written** (by John).*
- *The house **is being built**.*
- *The problem **has been solved**.*

### Tense in passive

Any tense can be passivized:

| Tense | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| Present simple | They make cars here. | Cars **are made** here. |
| Present continuous | They are building a house. | A house **is being built**. |
| Past simple | Shakespeare wrote Hamlet. | Hamlet **was written** by Shakespeare. |
| Present perfect | Someone has stolen my bike. | My bike **has been stolen**. |
| Future | They will announce it tomorrow. | It **will be announced** tomorrow. |
| Modals | You can see it from here. | It **can be seen** from here. |

### Get-passive (informal)

*Get* replaces *be* in informal contexts, often with adversity:

- *I **got fired**.* (I was fired.)
- *He **got hurt**.* (He was injured.)
- *They **got married**.* (They were married.)

### Agent with by

The doer is introduced with *by*:

- *The book **was written by** J.K. Rowling.*
- *The window **was broken by** the wind.*

When the agent is unknown or unimportant, omit it:

- *My car **was stolen**.* (I don't know who did it.)
- *Spanish **is spoken** here.* (The speakers are irrelevant.)

### Why use passive?

1. **Agent unknown**: *My bike was stolen.*
2. **Agent obvious/unimportant**: *The bill was passed.* (by Parliament — obvious)
3. **Focus on object**: *The window was broken.* (not who broke it)
4. **Formal/scientific style**: *It was observed that...*

## Chinese

### <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby>-passive

<ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> marks the passive. The agent (if mentioned) goes between <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> and the verb:

- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby><ruby>批<rt>pī</rt></ruby><ruby>评<rt>píng</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(He was criticized by the teacher.)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>车<rt>chē</rt></ruby><ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>偷<rt>tōu</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(My car was stolen.)

**Pattern**: Subject + <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> + (agent) + verb + <ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>

### Historical adversative tone

Traditionally, <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> implied something bad happened:

- <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>打<rt>dǎ</rt></ruby> (be beaten)
- <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>骂<rt>mà</rt></ruby> (be scolded)
- <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>骗<rt>piàn</rt></ruby> (be deceived)

Modern Chinese broadens this, but <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> still carries a slight passive-of-adversity flavor.

### Neutral alternatives

For neutral or positive passives, Chinese often uses active voice or other constructions:

- <ruby>由<rt>yóu</rt></ruby>...<ruby>做<rt>zuò</rt></ruby> (done by...) — formal, neutral
- <ruby>受<rt>shòu</rt></ruby>...<ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby><ruby>迎<rt>yíng</rt></ruby> (welcomed by...)
- Simply omit the agent: <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>本<rt>běn</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby><ruby>卖<rt>mài</rt></ruby><ruby>完<rt>wán</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(This book is sold out.)

### <ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby> / <ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby> as colloquial alternatives

<ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby> and <ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby> can replace <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> in informal speech:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby><ruby>骗<rt>piàn</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(I got scammed by someone.)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby><ruby>狗<rt>gǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>咬<rt>yǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(He got bitten by a dog.)

### <ruby>给<rt>gěi</rt></ruby> as informal passive

<ruby>给<rt>gěi</rt></ruby> in colloquial Northern Chinese:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>给<rt>gěi</rt></ruby><ruby>骂<rt>mà</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(I got scolded.)

### No verb change

Chinese verbs do not change form in the passive:

- <ruby>打<rt>dǎ</rt></ruby> (hit) → <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>打<rt>dǎ</rt></ruby> (be hit) — same verb

## Comparison at a glance

| Feature | Spanish | English | Chinese |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Standard passive | ser + participle | be + past participle | <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> + agent + verb |
| Participle agreement | Yes (gender/number) | No | No (verb unchanged) |
| Agent marker | por | by | <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> (agent between <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> and verb) |
| Frequency | Rare (prefer se or active) | Very common | Moderate (adversative) |
| Stative passive | estar + participle | be + participle (ambiguous) | <ruby>在<rt>zài</rt></ruby> / <ruby>着<rt>zháo</rt></ruby> |
| Informal passive | No equivalent | get-passive | <ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby> / <ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby> / <ruby>给<rt>gěi</rt></ruby> |
| Impersonal | se + 3rd person | It is + past participle | <ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby>... (someone...) |

## Common mistakes

1. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Overusing passive: *La decisión fue tomada* → *Se tomó la decisión* (impersonal se is more natural)

2. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Forgetting agreement: *La carta fue escrito* → *La carta fue **escrita***

3. **English speakers learning Chinese**: Using <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> for everything: *<ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>本<rt>běn</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby><ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>写<rt>xiě</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>* → *<ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>本<rt>běn</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby><ruby>作<rt>zuò</rt></ruby><ruby>者<rt>zhě</rt></ruby><ruby>写<rt>xiě</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby>* (active is more natural for neutral statements)

4. **Chinese speakers learning English**: Omitting 'be': *The letter written by him* → *The letter **was** written by him*
