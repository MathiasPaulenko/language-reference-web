## Overview

Sounds and interjections are among the most culturally variable elements of language.

- **Spanish**: Rich interjection system. Animal sounds often have more syllables than English. Regional variation is significant. Fillers include *este, pues, o sea*.
- **English**: Moderate onomatopoeia. Interjections range from mild (*gosh, gee*) to strong (*damn, hell*). Fillers (*um, uh, like, you know*) are stigmatized in formal speech.
- **Chinese**: Extensive onomatopoeic characters. Interjective particles modify sentence tone. Many characters exist solely for sound representation.

### Tone-bearing interjections

Chinese interjections change meaning with tone:

- <ruby>啊<rt>ā</rt></ruby> (first tone): surprise, realization
- <ruby>啊<rt>á</rt></ruby> (second tone): question, doubt
- <ruby>啊<rt>ǎ</rt></ruby> (third tone): surprise, response
- <ruby>啊<rt>à</rt></ruby> (fourth tone): understanding, agreement

## Common mistakes

1. **Using English sounds in other languages**: *The dog says woof* → In Spanish class, the dog says *guau*

2. **Translating interjections literally**: *¡Ay!* expresses pain, but *Oh!* in English is more surprise

3. **Chinese speakers learning English**: Using <ruby>嗯<rt>ňg</rt></ruby> excessively: *Um, I think um...* → reduce fillers in formal speech

4. **English speakers learning Chinese**: Not using <ruby>啊<rt>ā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>呀<rt>yā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby> appropriately: these particles are essential for natural Chinese
