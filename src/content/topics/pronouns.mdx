## Overview

Pronouns are words that take the place of nouns. Every language handles them differently, and these differences cause some of the most persistent mistakes for multilingual learners.

The three languages illustrate three very different philosophies:

- **Spanish**: Highly inflected. Verbs conjugate for person, number, tense, and mood, which allows pronoun dropping. Three levels of formality in second person.
- **English**: Minimal inflection. Pronouns are the main way to mark grammatical person. Gender distinction only in third person singular.
- **Chinese**: No inflection at all. Context, word order, and optional particles disambiguate meaning. One spoken form covers multiple written forms.

## Spanish

Spanish pronouns reflect grammatical person, number, gender, and formality.

### Personal pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | yo | nosotros/nosotras |
| 2nd (informal) | tú | vosotros/vosotras |
| 2nd (formal) | usted | ustedes |
| 3rd (masc) | él | ellos |
| 3rd (fem) | ella | ellas |

**Key rules:**
- **Subject pronouns are optional**: *Voy al mercado* (I go to the market) — the verb *voy* reveals the subject.
- **Three distances for demonstratives**: este (near speaker), ese (near listener), aquel (far from both).
- **Possessives agree with the possessed noun**, not the possessor: *su casa* (his/her/their house).

### Formality (tú vs usted)

| Context | Use |
|---------|-----|
| Friends, family, peers | tú |
| Elders, authority figures, strangers | usted |
| Regional variation | Latin America often uses ustedes for plural everywhere |

## English

English has one of the simplest pronoun systems among European languages.

### Personal pronouns

| Person | Subject | Object | Possessive |
|--------|---------|--------|------------|
| 1st sg | I | me | my/mine |
| 2nd | you | you | your/yours |
| 3rd masc | he | him | his |
| 3rd fem | she | her | hers |
| 3rd neut | it | it | its |
| 1st pl | we | us | our/ours |
| 3rd pl | they | them | their/theirs |

**Key rules:**
- **No formality distinction**: *you* covers singular/plural, formal/informal.
- **Only third person singular shows gender**: he/she/it.
- **Subject pronouns are mandatory**: *I go*, never just *Go* (unlike Spanish).
- **Possessive determiners vs pronouns**: *my book* vs *the book is mine*.

## Chinese

Chinese pronouns are remarkably simple in spoken form but have subtle written distinctions.

### Personal pronouns

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby> | <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby> |
| 2nd | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby> / <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby> (formal) | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby> |
| 3rd | <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby> / <ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby> / <ruby>它<rt>tā</rt></ruby> | <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby> |

**Key distinctions:**

| Character | Meaning | Pronunciation |
|---|---|---|
| <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby> | he / him | tā |
| <ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby> | she / her | tā |
| <ruby>它<rt>tā</rt></ruby> | it | tā |
| <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby> | you (formal/polite) | nín |

**Important**: Spoken Chinese does **not** distinguish gender in third person. All three are pronounced *tā*. Only writing reveals the gender.

### Possessives

Chinese uses a single particle <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> for all possession:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby> — my book
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>车<rt>chē</rt></ruby> — his car
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>家<rt>jiā</rt></ruby> — our home

**Omission rule**: With close relationships or inalienable possession, <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> can be dropped: <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>妈<rt>mā</rt></ruby> (my mom), not <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>妈<rt>mā</rt></ruby>.

### Demonstratives

| | This (singular) | These (plural) | That (singular) | Those (plural) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>ge</rt></ruby> | <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>些<rt>xiē</rt></ruby> | <ruby>那<rt>nà</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>ge</rt></ruby> | <ruby>那<rt>nà</rt></ruby><ruby>些<rt>xiē</rt></ruby> |

## Comparison at a glance

| Feature | Spanish | English | Chinese |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Subject pronoun dropping | Yes (verb conjugation reveals it) | No (always required) | No (context reveals it) |
| Formality levels | tú / usted / vosotros | you (all contexts) | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby> / <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby> |
| Gender in 3rd person | él/ella (spoken and written) | he/she/it (spoken and written) | <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>它<rt>tā</rt></ruby> (written only, all tā spoken) |
| Possessive structure | mi / mío / su | my / mine / his/hers | <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> particle (possessor + <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> + possessed) |
| Plural marker | built into pronoun | built into pronoun | <ruby>们<rt>mén</rt></ruby> suffix |
| Demonstrative distances | 3 (este/ese/aquel) | 2 (this/that) | 2 (<ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby>/<ruby>那<rt>nà</rt></ruby>) |

## Common mistakes

1. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Forgetting to drop subject pronouns (*Yo quiero* instead of *Quiero*) or using *tú* with strangers.

2. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Struggling with the lack of formality levels — using *you* for everyone feels either too distant or too familiar.

3. **English/Spanish speakers learning Chinese**: Confusing <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>她<rt>tā</rt></ruby>/<ruby>它<rt>tā</rt></ruby> in writing, or forgetting the possessive particle <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> (*<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby>* instead of *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby>*).

4. **Chinese speakers learning English**: Mixing up he/she because spoken Chinese uses tā for both, or omitting subject pronouns (*Is a student* instead of *He is a student*).
