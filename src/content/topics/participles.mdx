## Overview

Participles are non-finite verb forms that bridge the boundary between verbs and adjectives. They can describe ongoing actions (present participles) or completed states (past participles), and they frequently combine with auxiliary verbs to build compound tenses and passive constructions.

Spanish maintains a sharp distinction between the gerundio (ongoing action) and the participio (completed state), both with regular and irregular forms. English uses -ing and -ed/-en forms across a wider range of functions, including as nouns (gerunds). Chinese, as an analytic language with no verb inflection, expresses the same conceptual distinctions through aspect particles and resultative verb compounds rather than morphological change.

Comparing how these three languages encode what participles express in Indo-European grammar reveals fundamental differences in how tense, aspect, and voice are packaged into words.

## Explanation

### Spanish

Spanish has two participial forms:

- **Gerundio** (present participle): formed with **-ando** (-ar verbs) and **-iendo** (-er/-ir verbs). Used exclusively in progressive tenses: *Estoy leyendo* (I am reading). Never used as a noun or adjective.
- **Participio** (past participle): regular forms end in **-ado** (-ar) and **-ido** (-er/-ir). Used in compound tenses (*He leído*) and as adjectives (*La puerta está abierta*).

Key irregular past participles: *escrito* (escribir), *visto* (ver), *hecho* (hacer), *muerto* (morir), *abierto* (abrir), *puesto* (poner).

### English

English participles are highly versatile:

- **Present participle** (-ing): forms progressive tenses (*is running*), acts as adjective (*a running river*), and can introduce reduced clauses (*Running late, she called*). The same -ing form also functions as a gerund (noun): *Swimming is healthy*.
- **Past participle** (-ed/-en): forms perfect tenses (*has walked*), passives (*was built*), and adjectives (*a broken window*). Regular verbs add -ed; irregular verbs vary (*written, seen, gone, broken*).

Adjectival participles often come in pairs with distinct meaning: *boring* (cause) vs *bored* (effect); *interesting* vs *interested*.

### Chinese

Chinese has no category called "participle." Instead:

- **Progressive aspect**: <ruby>正<rt>zhèng</rt></ruby><ruby>在<rt>zài</rt></ruby> + verb (<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>正<rt>zhèng</rt></ruby><ruby>在<rt>zài</rt></ruby><ruby>看<rt>kàn</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby>, I am reading).
- **Experiential perfect**: verb + <ruby>过<rt>guò</rt></ruby> (<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>看<rt>kàn</rt></ruby><ruby>过<rt>guò</rt></ruby><ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>部<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>电<rt>diàn</rt></ruby><ruby>影<rt>yǐng</rt></ruby>, I have seen this film).
- **Resultative complements**: verb + result (<ruby>打<rt>dǎ</rt></ruby><ruby>开<rt>kāi</rt></ruby> "open-[achieve]", <ruby>看<rt>kàn</rt></ruby><ruby>见<rt>jiàn</rt></ruby> "see-[achieve]").
- **Passive**: <ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> + agent + verb (<ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby><ruby>被<rt>bèi</rt></ruby><ruby>丢<rt>diū</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>, The book was lost).
- **Attributive clauses**: use <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> after a verb phrase: <ruby>跑<rt>pǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>步<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> (the person [who is] running).

Because Chinese verbs do not inflect, all of these meanings are expressed through separate words (particles, auxiliaries, or compounding) rather than verb form changes.
