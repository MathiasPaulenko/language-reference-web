## Overview

Negation turns a positive statement into a negative one. Each language has its own mechanics.

- **Spanish**: Places *no* directly before the verb. Double negatives are not only allowed but common. Negative pronouns (*nada, nadie, ninguno*) reinforce rather than cancel negation.
- **English**: Requires the auxiliary *do* for most verbs. Double negatives are ungrammatical. Negative concord does not exist.
- **Chinese**: Places <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> or <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> before the verb. No auxiliary needed. The choice between <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> and <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> depends on aspect (habitual vs completed).

<div class="spanish-section">

## Spanish

Spanish negation is straightforward: put **no** before the verb.

### Simple negation

- *No vengo.* (I don't come.)
- *No es verdad.* (It's not true.)
- *No tienen dinero.* (They don't have money.)

### Double negatives

Spanish **reinforces** negation with additional negative words:

- *No veo **nada**.* (I don't see anything. — lit. "I don't see nothing")
- *No viene **nadie**.* (Nobody is coming. — lit. "Not nobody comes")
- *No tengo **ningún** problema.* (I don't have any problem.)

**Rule**: Once the sentence is negative with *no*, all indefinite pronouns become negative.

### Negative pronouns

| Affirmative | Negative | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| algo | nada | nothing |
| alguien | nadie | nobody |
| alguno | ninguno | none |
| siempre | nunca | never |
| también | tampoco | neither |

### Negation without no

In replies, *no* can be omitted:

- *¿Vienes?* — *Nunca.* (Are you coming? — Never.)
- *¿Tienes algo?* — *Nada.* (Do you have anything? — Nothing.)

## English

English negation is more complex because it requires **auxiliary support**.

### Simple negation with do

For most verbs, use *do/does/did + not*:

- *I **do not** like coffee.*
- *She **does not** speak Spanish.*
- *They **did not** arrive on time.*

### Be and modals (no do needed)

With *be* and modal verbs, negate directly:

- *I **am not** tired.*
- *She **cannot** swim.*
- *They **will not** come.*

### Negative pronouns

| Pronoun | Example |
|---|---|
| nothing | *I know **nothing**.* |
| nobody / no one | **Nobody** came. |
| none | *I want **none**.* |
| never | *I **never** eat meat.* |
| neither | *I like **neither** option.* |

### No double negatives

English does **not** allow double negatives:

- ❌ *I don't know nothing.* → ✅ *I don't know **anything**.*
- ❌ *Nobody never came.* → ✅ *Nobody **ever** came.*

The second negative cancels the first, creating a logical positive — which is not what the speaker intends.

### Negative questions

Negative questions expect confirmation:

- *Aren't you coming?* (I thought you were.)
- *Didn't she call?* (I expected her to.)

## Chinese

Chinese negation uses **two particles**: <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> and <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby>. No auxiliary verb needed.

### <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> — habitual, future, or general negation

<ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> negates actions that are habitual, intentional, or in the future:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huan</rt></ruby><ruby>咖<rt>kā</rt></ruby><ruby>啡<rt>fēi</rt></ruby>。(I don't like coffee. — general/habitual)
- <ruby>明<rt>míng</rt></ruby><ruby>天<rt>tiān</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby>。(Tomorrow I'm not going. — future)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby><ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby>。(He is not a teacher. — copula negation)

### <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> — completed actions or existence

<ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> negates actions that were supposed to happen but didn't, or denies existence:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby><ruby>早<rt>zǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>饭<rt>fàn</rt></ruby>。(I didn't eat breakfast. — past completion)
- <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby>。(There is nobody. — existence)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby>。(He didn't come. — expected but didn't happen)

### <ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby> with negation

<ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby> (completion) is **not** used with <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby>, because <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> already implies non-completion:

- ❌ <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby> → ✅ <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby>

### Negative pronouns

| Affirmative | Negative | Example |
|---|---|---|
| <ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>什<rt>shí</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>mó</rt></ruby> | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>什<rt>shén</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>me</rt></ruby> | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>什<rt>shén</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>me</rt></ruby><ruby>问<rt>wèn</rt></ruby><ruby>题<rt>tí</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>总<rt>zǒng</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby> | <ruby>从<rt>cóng</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> / <ruby>从<rt>cóng</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> | <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>从<rt>cóng</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>抽<rt>chōu</rt></ruby><ruby>烟<rt>yān</rt></ruby> |

### Double negation in Chinese

Chinese double negation creates a positive meaning (like English):

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>。(It's not that I don't know. → I do know.)

## Comparison at a glance

| Feature | Spanish | English | Chinese |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Simple negation | no + verb | do + not + verb | <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby>/<ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> + verb |
| Auxiliary needed | No | Yes (do/be/modal) | No |
| Double negative | Reinforces | Cancels (ungrammatical) | Cancels (positive) |
| Negative pronouns | nada, nadie, ninguno | nothing, nobody, none | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby>, <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby><ruby>什<rt>shí</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>mó</rt></ruby> |
| Negation of be | no + ser/estar | am/is/are + not | <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby> |
| Negation of have | no + tener | do/does + not + have | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby> |
| Past negation | no + preterite/imperfect | did + not + verb | <ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby> + verb |

## Common mistakes

1. **Spanish/Chinese speakers learning English**: Forgetting 'do' in negation: *I not like* → *I do not like*

2. **English/Spanish speakers learning Chinese**: Using <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> for everything: *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby>* (I won't eat) when they mean *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby>* (I didn't eat)

3. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Double negatives: *I don't know nothing* → *I don't know anything*

4. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Avoiding double negatives: *No veo algo* → *No veo nada*
