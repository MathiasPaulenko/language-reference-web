## Overview

Topic-comment structure organizes sentences as "what I'm talking about" + "what I say about it."

- **Spanish**: Subject-prominent with SVO order. Allows fronting for emphasis, but the grammatical subject remains fixed. Object fronting uses clitic pronouns (*A Juan **lo** vi*). Pro-drop allows subject omission.
- **English**: Strictly subject-prominent with rigid SVO. Fronting is possible but marked and often requires special intonation. Subject omission is very limited (imperatives, diary style, informal responses).
- **Chinese**: Topic-prominent. The topic (not necessarily the grammatical subject) comes first. Comment follows. Subject omission is extremely common. No grammatical marking needed for topic fronting.

## Spanish

### Subject-prominent SVO

Spanish default order is subject-verb-object:

- *Yo leo libros.* (I read books.)
- *María compró una casa.* (María bought a house.)

### Fronting for emphasis

Objects and adverbs can be fronted:

- *A María **la** vi ayer.* (María, I saw her yesterday. — object fronted with clitic)
- *Ese libro **lo** tengo.* (That book, I have it.)
- *Rápidamente **lo** hizo.* (Quickly, he did it.)

**Rule**: Fronted objects require a clitic pronoun (*lo, la, los, las, le, les, me, te, nos, os*).

### Pro-drop

Spanish omits subjects freely when clear from context:

- *(Yo) Voy a casa.* (I'm going home.)
- *(Él) Está cansado.* (He's tired.)
- *(Nosotros) Comemos tarde.* (We eat late.)

### En cuanto a / respecto a (topic markers)

Formal ways to introduce a topic:

- *En cuanto a **el dinero**, no te preocupes.* (As for the money, don't worry.)
- *Respecto a **tu pregunta**, no lo sé.* (Regarding your question, I don't know.)
- *Por lo que respecta a **Juan**, está bien.* (As for Juan, he's fine.)

### Left-dislocation

A topic is placed at the beginning, then resumed with a pronoun:

- *Juan, **lo** conozco bien.* (Juan, I know him well.)
- *El dinero, **lo** tengo.* (The money, I have it.)

### Passive vs topic-comment

Spanish prefers impersonal or active constructions:

- *Se venden casas aquí.* (Houses are sold here. — impersonal)
- *Las casas **se** venden rápido.* (Houses sell quickly. — passive-reflexive)

## English

### Strict SVO

English requires subject-verb-object order:

- *I read books.*
- *María bought a house.*

Deviations are marked and require specific contexts.

### Fronting (marked)

Fronting is possible but sounds emphatic or literary:

- *That book, I have read.* (marked, contrastive)
- *Him I trust.* (emphatic: others, maybe not)
- *Quickly he ran.* (literary/narrative)

**Rule**: Fronting in English is **not neutral**. It signals contrast or special focus.

### As for / regarding (topic markers)

- *As for **the money**, don't worry.*
- *Regarding **your question**, I'm not sure.*
- *When it comes to **sports**, he's clueless.*
- *Speaking of **Juan**, have you seen him?*

### Subject omission (very limited)

- *Hope you're well.* (informal, message style)
- *Can't make it.* (informal)
- *See you tomorrow.* (imperative-like)
- *Nice to meet you.* (fixed expression)

**Diary style**: *Woke up late. Ate breakfast. Went to work.* (I omitted)

### Passive for topic-prominence

English uses the passive to bring the object to topic position:

- *The house **was bought** by María.* (topic: house)
- *Houses **are sold** here.* (topic: houses)
- *It **is believed** that he left.* (impersonal topic: it)

## Chinese

### Topic-comment as default

Chinese sentences naturally begin with the topic:

- <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>ge</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>。(This, I know. — topic = this; comment = I know)
- <ruby>钱<rt>qián</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby>，<ruby>时<rt>shí</rt></ruby><ruby>间<rt>jiān</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby>。(Money I have; time I don't. — two topics)
- <ruby>中<rt>zhōng</rt></ruby><ruby>文<rt>wén</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby><ruby>三<rt>sān</rt></ruby><ruby>年<rt>nián</rt></ruby>。(Chinese, I've studied for three years.)

### No topic marker needed

Chinese topics simply appear at the beginning. No special grammar:

- <ruby>鱼<rt>yú</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby>。(Fish, I don't eat.)
- <ruby>北<rt>běi</rt></ruby><ruby>京<rt>jīng</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby><ruby>过<rt>guò</rt></ruby>。(Beijing, I've been to.)

### <ruby>呢<rt>ne</rt></ruby> for topic shift

<ruby>呢<rt>ne</rt></ruby> shifts to a new topic or asks about a previous one:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>。<ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>呢<rt>ne</rt></ruby>？(I'm fine. And you?)
- <ruby>至<rt>zhì</rt></ruby><ruby>于<rt>yú</rt></ruby><ruby>钱<rt>qián</rt></ruby>，<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>。(As for money, I don't know.)

### Subject omission

Chinese omits subjects far more freely than English:

- <ruby>吃<rt>chī</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>？(Eaten? — "Have you eaten?")
- <ruby>去<rt>qù</rt></ruby><ruby>哪<rt>nǎ</rt></ruby><ruby>儿<rt>er</rt></ruby>？(Going where? — "Where are you going?")
- <ruby>太<rt>tài</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>！(Great! — "That's great!")

### Topic as frame

The topic sets the frame; the comment provides new information:

- <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>ge</rt></ruby><ruby>问<rt>wèn</rt></ruby><ruby>题<rt>tí</rt></ruby>，<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby><ruby>明<rt>míng</rt></ruby><ruby>天<rt>tiān</rt></ruby><ruby>再<rt>zài</rt></ruby><ruby>讨<rt>tǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>论<rt>lùn</rt></ruby>。(This problem, we'll discuss tomorrow.)
- <ruby>那<rt>nà</rt></ruby><ruby>件<rt>jiàn</rt></ruby><ruby>事<rt>shì</rt></ruby>，<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>已<rt>yǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>经<rt>jīng</rt></ruby><ruby>忘<rt>wàng</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(That matter, I've already forgotten.)

### Double subject construction

Chinese allows "topic + subject + predicate":

- <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>本<rt>běn</rt></ruby><ruby>书<rt>shū</rt></ruby><ruby>内<rt>nèi</rt></ruby><ruby>容<rt>róng</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>。(This book, the content is very good. — book = topic; content = subject)
- <ruby>北<rt>běi</rt></ruby><ruby>京<rt>jīng</rt></ruby><ruby>天<rt>tiān</rt></ruby><ruby>气<rt>qì</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>。(Beijing, the weather is good. — Beijing = topic; weather = subject)

## Common mistakes

1. **Chinese speakers learning English**: Dropping subjects: *Like it* → *I like it*

2. **English speakers learning Chinese**: Overusing SVO: *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby><ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>gè</rt></ruby>* is correct but *<ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>gè</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby>* is often more natural for contrast

3. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Fronting without marking: *The book I have read* sounds literary; *I have read the book* is neutral

4. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Fronting without clitic: *A María vi ayer* → *A María **la** vi ayer*
