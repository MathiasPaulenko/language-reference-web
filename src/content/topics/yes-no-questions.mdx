---## Overview

Yes-no questions expect a simple confirmation or denial. The three languages use fundamentally different strategies.

- **English**: Grammaticalized auxiliary inversion (Are you...?) or rising intonation for informal speech.
- **Spanish**: Flexible word order with obligatory question marks; intonation carries the primary interrogative force.
- **Chinese**: Dedicated particle <ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby> transforms a statement into a question without changing word order.

## Overview

Yes-no questions (polar questions) seek confirmation or denial. English uses auxiliary inversion or rising intonation. Spanish uses rising intonation, inversion, or the tag verdad. Chinese adds the particle <ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby><rt>ma</rt></ruby> at the end of a statement or uses the affirmative-negative verb construction.

The three languages approach this grammatical concept in different ways that reflect their underlying systems. A detailed comparison follows.

## English

English yes-no questions require auxiliary movement:

- **With be/modals**: Invert subject and auxiliary — *Are you ready? Can she swim?*
- **With lexical verbs**: Add do-support — *Do you like coffee? Did he arrive?*
- **Intonation only**: *You're coming?* (rising pitch, no grammar change)

Negative questions carry a bias:
- *Aren't you coming?* = I expected you to come
- *Don't you like it?* = I thought you would like it

---

## Spanish

Spanish yes-no questions have more flexibility:

- **Intonation alone**: *¿Tú vienes?* (same word order as statement, rising intonation)
- **Inversion**: *¿Vienes tú?* (verb-subject, more emphatic)
- **With tags**: *¿Vienes, verdad?* / *¿Vienes, no?*
- **Negative**: *¿No vienes?* (assumes the negative)

**Written Spanish** requires ¿ at the start and ? at the end.

---

## Chinese

Chinese has three distinct yes-no question strategies:

1. **Particle <ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>**: Statement + <ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>
   - <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>？(How are you? / Are you well?)
   - <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby><ruby>咖<rt>kā</rt></ruby><ruby>啡<rt>fēi</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>？(Do you like coffee?)

2. **Affirmative-negative (A-not-A)**: Verb + <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby> + verb
   - <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby>？(Are you coming or not?)
   - <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>个<rt>gè</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>？(Is this good or not?)

3. **Intonation alone**: Rising pitch on a statement (informal)
   - <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>？(You've come?)

- <ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby> is never used with question words (<ruby>什<rt>shí</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>mó</rt></ruby>, <ruby>谁<rt>shuí</rt></ruby>, <ruby>哪<rt>nǎ</rt></ruby><ruby>里<rt>lǐ</rt></ruby>).
- A-not-A questions are more direct and expect a decisive answer.

## Answering yes-no questions

| Language | Positive | Negative | Notes |
|----------|----------|----------|-------|
| English | Yes, I am / Yes, I do | No, I'm not / No, I don't | Must match the auxiliary |
| Spanish | Sí / Sí, vengo | No / No, no vengo | Can repeat the verb for emphasis |
| Chinese | <ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby>/<ruby>对<rt>duì</rt></ruby>/<ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>/<ruby>嗯<rt>ňg</rt></ruby> | <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby>/<ruby>没<rt>méi</rt></ruby><ruby>有<rt>yǒu</rt></ruby> | Echo the verb or use generic particles |

Chinese answers often echo the main verb rather than using a generic yes/no:
- <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby>？<ruby>喜<rt>xǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>欢<rt>huān</rt></ruby>。(Do you like it? Like.)
- <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>饿<rt>è</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>饿<rt>è</rt></ruby>？<ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>饿<rt>è</rt></ruby>。(Are you hungry? Not hungry.)
