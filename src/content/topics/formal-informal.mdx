## Overview

Every language encodes social distance and politeness, but the mechanisms differ dramatically.

- **Spanish**: Has a **T-V distinction** (tú vs usted) with separate verb conjugations. Regional variation is significant (vos in Argentina, vosotros in Spain). Using the wrong form can be offensive or overly distant.
- **English**: **No pronoun distinction** for formality. Politeness is expressed through modal verbs (*could* vs *can*), hedging (*I was wondering if...*), titles (*Mr.*, *Dr.*), and intonation. The same "you" addresses a child or a president.
- **Chinese**: Uses **<ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby>** (nín) as the formal "you." Honorific titles are extremely important. Sentence-final particles (*<ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby>, <ruby>啊<rt>ā</rt></ruby>, <ruby>嘛<rt>má</rt></ruby>*) soften requests. The <ruby>把<rt>bǎ</rt></ruby> construction and <ruby>请<rt>qǐng</rt></ruby> mark politeness.

## Spanish

### Tú vs usted

| | tú | usted |
|---|---|---|
| Usage | Friends, family, children, peers | Strangers, elders, authority, formal contexts |
| Verb ending | -as / -es | -a / -e |
| Example | *¿Cómo **estás**?* | *¿Cómo **está** (usted)?* |
| Pronouns | te, tu, tuyo | le, su, suyo |

**Examples**:

- *¿**De dónde eres** (tú)?* (Where are you from? — friend)
- *¿**De dónde es** (usted)?* (Where are you from? — stranger)
- ***Pasa** (tú).* (Come in. — friend)
- ***Pase** (usted).* (Come in. — stranger)

### Vos (Rioplatense)

In Argentina, Uruguay, and parts of Central America, *vos* replaces *tú*:

- *¿Cómo **andás**?* (How are you? — vos form)
- *Vos **sos** mi amigo.* (You are my friend.)

**Vos conjugation**: Stress on the final syllable:

| Infinitive | tú | vos |
|---|---|---|
| hablar | hablas | hablás |
| comer | comes | comés |
| vivir | vives | vivís |
| tener | tienes | tenés |
| ir | vas | vas |
| ser | eres | sos |

### Vosotros (Spain)

Spain uses *vosotros* for informal plural "you" and *ustedes* for formal plural:

- *¿Cómo **estáis** (vosotros)?* (How are you all? — friends, Spain)
- *¿Cómo **están** (ustedes)?* (How are you all? — formal, or Latin America)

**Latin America**: *Ustedes* is used for **both** formal and informal plural.

### Title conventions

| Title | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|
| señor / Sr. | Mr. | *Señor García* |
| señora / Sra. | Mrs. / Ms. | *Señora López* |
| señorita / Srta. | Miss (young/unmarried) | *Señorita Martínez* |
| don / doña | honorific + first name | *Don Miguel, Doña Elena* |
| doctor / doctora | PhD / MD | *Doctora Sánchez* |
| ingeniero / -a | engineer | *Ingeniero Ruiz* |

**Note**: *Don/Doña* is still used in formal and rural contexts. *Señorita* is falling out of favor.

### Formal register markers

| Informal | Formal |
|---|---|
| *¿Qué tal?* | *¿Cómo está (usted)?* |
| *¡Hola!* | *Buenos días / tardes* |
| *¿Puedes...?* | *¿Podría usted...?* |
| *Ven aquí.* | *Venga usted, por favor.* |
| *Dime.* | *¿Me lo podría decir?* |
| *Gracias.* | *Muchas gracias / Le agradezco.* |

### Commands and formality

- *Tú: **Ven**.* (Come.)
- *Usted: **Venga**.* (Come.)
- *Vosotros: **Venid**.* (Come [all]. — Spain)
- *Ustedes: **Vengan**.* (Come [all].)

## English

### No T-V distinction

English uses *you* for everyone — intimate, formal, singular, plural. Politeness is expressed through **other means**.

### Modal verbs for politeness

| Direct | Polite |
|---|---|
| *Can you help me?* | *Could you help me?* |
| *I want to see the menu.* | *I would like to see the menu.* |
| *Tell me the price.* | *Could you tell me the price?* |
| *Open the door.* | *Would you mind opening the door?* |
| *I need this by Friday.* | *I was wondering if I could get this by Friday.* |

### Titles

| Title | Usage |
|---|---|
| Mr. + surname | Adult male (neutral) |
| Mrs. + surname | Married woman |
| Ms. + surname | Woman (marital status unknown) |
| Miss + surname | Young woman (falling out of favor) |
| Dr. + surname | PhD or MD |
| Prof. + surname | Professor |
| Sir / Ma'am | General respect (service contexts) |

**Note**: First-name basis is common in American workplaces, even with superiors. In British English, surnames often persist longer.

### Hedging and indirectness

English softens requests through circumlocution:

- *I was **wondering** if you could...*
- *I was **hoping** to...*
- *Do you think you might be able to...?*
- *Would it be possible to...?*
- *I don't suppose you could...?*

### Informal markers

| Formal | Informal |
|---|---|
| *Hello* | *Hi / Hey* |
| *Goodbye* | *Bye / See ya* |
| *Thank you very much* | *Thanks / Cheers* |
| *I would like* | *I want / I'd like* |
| *Children* | *Kids* |
| *Request* | *Ask for* |

### Regional plural you

English lacks an official plural "you," so regions have filled the gap:

- *you guys* (US, general)
- *y'all* (Southern US)
- *you all* (Southern US, full form)
- *yinz* (Pittsburgh)
- *ye* (Ireland)

## Chinese

### <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby> vs <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby>

| | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby> (nǐ) | <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby> (nín) |
|---|---|---|
| Usage | Neutral, peers, subordinates, children | Elders, superiors, customers, strangers |
| Tone | Casual | Respectful |
| Example | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby> (hello) | <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby> (hello, respectful) |

**Examples**:

- <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby><ruby>什<rt>shén</rt></ruby><ruby>么<rt>me</rt></ruby><ruby>名<rt>míng</rt></ruby><ruby>字<rt>zi</rt></ruby>？(What's your name? — peer)
- <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>贵<rt>guì</rt></ruby><ruby>姓<rt>xìng</rt></ruby>？(What's your surname? — formal, respectful)

### Honorific titles

Chinese titles are essential for proper address:

| Title | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|
| <ruby>先<rt>xiān</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>shēng</rt></ruby> | Mr. / teacher / master | <ruby>王<rt>Wáng</rt></ruby><ruby>先<rt>xiān</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>shēng</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>女<rt>nǚ</rt></ruby><ruby>士<rt>shì</rt></ruby> | Ms. / Mrs. (modern) | <ruby>李<rt>Lǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>女<rt>nǚ</rt></ruby><ruby>士<rt>shì</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby> | Teacher (used broadly for professionals) | <ruby>张<rt>Zhāng</rt></ruby><ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>医<rt>yī</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>shēng</rt></ruby> | Doctor | <ruby>陈<rt>Chén</rt></ruby><ruby>医<rt>yī</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>shēng</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>同<rt>tóng</rt></ruby><ruby>志<rt>zhì</rt></ruby> | Comrade (dated, official) | — |
| <ruby>小<rt>xiǎo</rt></ruby> + surname | Young [surname] (casual, for younger) | <ruby>小<rt>Xiǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>王<rt>Wáng</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby> + surname | Old [surname] (casual, for older/familiar) | <ruby>老<rt>Lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>李<rt>Lǐ</rt></ruby> |

**Important**: Using <ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby> for anyone with expertise (writers, musicians, etc.) is common and respectful.

### Request politeness strategies

| Strategy | Example |
|---|---|
| <ruby>请<rt>qǐng</rt></ruby> (please) | <ruby>请<rt>qǐng</rt></ruby><ruby>坐<rt>zuò</rt></ruby> (please sit) |
| <ruby>麻<rt>má</rt></ruby><ruby>烦<rt>fán</rt></ruby> (trouble) | <ruby>麻<rt>má</rt></ruby><ruby>烦<rt>fan</rt></ruby><ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>帮<rt>bāng</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby> (trouble you to help me) |
| <ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby> (can or cannot) | <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby>... (can you or not...) |
| <ruby>一<rt>yī</rt></ruby><ruby>下<rt>xià</rt></ruby> (a bit) | <ruby>帮<rt>bāng</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>一<rt>yí</rt></ruby><ruby>下<rt>xià</rt></ruby> (help me a bit) |
| <ruby>吧<rt>bā</rt></ruby> (softening) | <ruby>给<rt>gěi</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>吧<rt>ba</rt></ruby> (give it to me [softened]) |
| <ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby> (is it okay?) | <ruby>这<rt>zhè</rt></ruby><ruby>样<rt>yàng</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>吗<rt>ma</rt></ruby> (is this okay?) |

### Giving and receiving face

Chinese politeness is deeply tied to <ruby>面<rt>miàn</rt></ruby><ruby>子<rt>zǐ</rt></ruby> (*miànzi*, face):

- Giving compliments: <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>太<rt>tài</rt></ruby><ruby>客<rt>kè</rt></ruby><ruby>气<rt>qi</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby> (You're too polite — deflecting praise)
- Declining offers initially: <ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>用<rt>yòng</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>用<rt>yòng</rt></ruby> (No need, no need — may accept after insistence)
- Humble self-reference: <ruby>晚<rt>wǎn</rt></ruby><ruby>辈<rt>bèi</rt></ruby> (this junior), <ruby>鄙<rt>bǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>人<rt>rén</rt></ruby> (this unworthy person — very formal)

### <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>mén</rt></ruby> / <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>mén</rt></ruby>

- <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby> (you plural — neutral)
- <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>men</rt></ruby> (you plural formal — rare, mostly northern China)

In practice, <ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>们<rt>mén</rt></ruby> is uncommon. Speakers use <ruby>各<rt>gè</rt></ruby><ruby>位<rt>wèi</rt></ruby> (everyone) or <ruby>大<rt>dà</rt></ruby><ruby>家<rt>jiā</rt></ruby> (everybody) instead:

- <ruby>各<rt>gè</rt></ruby><ruby>位<rt>wèi</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby> (Hello everyone [formal].)

## Common mistakes

1. **English speakers learning Spanish**: Using tú with everyone: *¿Cómo estás, señor?* → *¿Cómo **está** (usted), señor?*

2. **Spanish speakers learning English**: Overformalizing: *"Do you want" is fine between friends; not everything needs "Would you like"*

3. **English speakers learning Chinese**: Using <ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby> for teachers/elders: *<ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>，<ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby>* → *<ruby>您<rt>nín</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>，<ruby>老<rt>lǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>师<rt>shī</rt></ruby>*

4. **Spanish speakers in Latin America**: Using vosotros: *¿Cómo estáis?* → *¿Cómo **están**?*
