## Overview

Complement clauses are subordinate clauses that function as noun phrases: subjects, objects, or predicative complements.

- **Spanish**: Uses *que* to introduce complement clauses. The subjunctive is required after verbs of volition, emotion, doubt, and necessity. Indicative is used after verbs of knowledge, perception, and certainty.
- **English**: Uses *that*-clauses, infinitive clauses, and *-ing* clauses. *That* is often deletable in informal contexts. Extraposition (moving a heavy subject to the end) is common.
- **Chinese**: Uses the clause directly as subject or object without special marking. *<ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby>* introduces reported speech. *<ruby>觉<rt>jué</rt></ruby><ruby>得<rt>dé</rt></ruby>/<ruby>认<rt>rèn</rt></ruby><ruby>为<rt>wéi</rt></ruby>/<ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>* take bare clauses as objects.

## Spanish

### Que + indicative (factual complements)

After verbs of knowledge, perception, certainty:

- *Sé **que** vino.* (I know that he came.)
- *Veo **que** está cansado.* (I see that he is tired.)
- *Creo **que** tiene razón.* (I think he is right.)
- *Dijo **que** vendría.* (He said he would come.)
- *Me parece **que** es tarde.* (It seems to me that it's late.)

### Que + subjunctive (non-factual complements)

After verbs of desire, command, emotion, doubt, necessity:

**Desire**:
- *Quiero **que** vengas.* (I want you to come.)
- *Espero **que** llegues pronto.* (I hope you arrive soon.)

**Command**:
- *Mando **que** lo hagas.* (I order you to do it.)
- *Insisto en **que** estudies.* (I insist that you study.)

**Emotion**:
- *Me alegro **de que** hayas venido.* (I'm glad you came.)
- *Siento **que** estés enfermo.* (I'm sorry you're sick.)

**Doubt**:
- *Dudo **que** sea verdad.* (I doubt it's true.)
- *No creo **que** venga.* (I don't think he'll come.)

**Necessity**:
- *Es necesario **que** estudies.* (It's necessary that you study.)
- *Conviene **que** vengas.* (It's advisable that you come.)

### Infinitive complement (same subject)

When the subject of the main and subordinate clause is the same:

- *Quiero **ir**.* (I want to go.)
- *Espero **llegar** pronto.* (I hope to arrive soon.)
- *Es importante **estudiar**.* (It's important to study.)
- *Me alegra **verte**.* (I'm glad to see you.)

### Subject complement clause

Spanish allows pre-verbal subject clauses naturally:

- ***Que** llueva es triste.* (That it's raining is sad.)
- ***Que** no venga me preocupa.* (That he's not coming worries me.)

But extraposition is also common:

- *Es triste **que** llueva.* (It's sad that it's raining.)

### De que vs que

After certain nouns and verbs, *de* is required:

- *Me alegro **de que** vengas.* (I'm glad that you're coming.)
- *Tengo miedo **de que** no llegue.* (I'm afraid he won't arrive.)
- *La posibilidad **de que** llueva* (the possibility that it will rain)

## English

### That-clauses

**As object**:

- *I know **that** he came.*
- *She believes **that** he is right.*
- *He said **that** he was tired.*

**That-deletion** (informal):

- *I know Ø he came.*
- *She believes Ø he is right.*
- *He said Ø he was tired.*

**Note**: *That* cannot be deleted after certain verbs:

- ❌ *I replied Ø he was wrong.* → *I replied **that** he was wrong.*

### As subject (extraposition)

English prefers extraposition for subject clauses:

- ✅ *It's important **that** he study.* (extraposed)
- ? *That he study is important.* (pre-verbal, awkward)

- *It's sad **that** she left.*
- *It's likely **that** he'll come.*
- *It seems **that** he knows.*

### Infinitive complements

**After want, need, hope, decide, etc.**:

- *I want **to leave**.*
- *She decided **to stay**.*
- *He hopes **to win**.*

**With object + infinitive**:

- *I want **him to leave**.*
- *She asked **me to help**.*

### -ing complements

- *I enjoy **reading**.*
- *She admitted **stealing** it.*
- *He suggested **going** early.*

### Wh-clauses

- *I don't know **what to do**.*
- *She understands **how it works**.*
- *I wonder **whether he will come**.*

### For + to infinitive

- *It's important **for him to study**.*
- *I arranged **for her to meet** us.*

## Chinese

### Bare clause as object

Chinese complement clauses appear without special marking:

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(I know he came.)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>觉<rt>jué</rt></ruby><ruby>得<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>聪<rt>cōng</rt></ruby><ruby>明<rt>ming</rt></ruby>。(I think he is smart.)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>认<rt>rèn</rt></ruby><ruby>为<rt>wéi</rt></ruby><ruby>你<rt>nǐ</rt></ruby><ruby>对<rt>duì</rt></ruby>。(I think you're right.)

### <ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby> for reported speech

<ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby> introduces reported speech:

- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>累<rt>lèi</rt></ruby>。(He said he was tired.)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>。(He said he wasn't coming.)

**Note**: <ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby> is used only for speech verbs. For think/believe/know, it is omitted.

### <ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby> nominalization

- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>事<rt>shì</rt></ruby><ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>高<rt>gāo</rt></ruby><ruby>兴<rt>xìng</rt></ruby>。(His coming made me happy.)
- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby><ruby>的<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>是<rt>shì</rt></ruby>... (What I know is...)

### Subject clause (pre-verbal)

Chinese allows subject clauses naturally:

- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>不<rt>bù</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>担<rt>dān</rt></ruby><ruby>心<rt>xīn</rt></ruby>。(That he's not coming worries me.)
- <ruby>学<rt>xué</rt></ruby><ruby>习<rt>xí</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>重<rt>zhòng</rt></ruby><ruby>要<rt>yào</rt></ruby>。(Studying is important. — verb as subject)

### <ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby>/<ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby>/<ruby>使<rt>shǐ</rt></ruby> (causative complement)

- <ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>让<rt>ràng</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby>。(I made him come.)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>叫<rt>jiào</rt></ruby><ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>走<rt>zǒu</rt></ruby>。(He told me to leave.)

### <ruby>得<rt>dé</rt></ruby> as complement marker

- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby><ruby>得<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>好<rt>hǎo</rt></ruby>。(He speaks very well. — complement of manner)
- <ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>跑<rt>pǎo</rt></ruby><ruby>得<rt>de</rt></ruby><ruby>很<rt>hěn</rt></ruby><ruby>快<rt>kuài</rt></ruby>。(He runs very fast.)

| Function | Spanish | English | Chinese |
|----------|---------|---------|---------|

| Non-factual object clause | que + subjunctive | that + clause / infinitive | bare clause + <ruby>能<rt>néng</rt></ruby>/<ruby>会<rt>huì</rt></ruby> |
| Same subject infinitive | infinitive | to + infinitive | bare verb |
| Subject clause | que + clause | extraposed it + that-clause | clause directly as subject |

## Common mistakes

1. **English speakers learning Spanish**: *Quiero que vienes* → *Quiero que **vengas*** (subjunctive)

2. **Spanish speakers learning English**: *I want that you come* → *I want you **to come***

3. **English speakers learning Chinese**: *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby><ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby><ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>* → *<ruby>我<rt>wǒ</rt></ruby><ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>**<ruby>他<rt>tā</rt></ruby><ruby>来<rt>lái</rt></ruby><ruby>了<rt>le</rt></ruby>*** (no <ruby>说<rt>shuō</rt></ruby> with <ruby>知<rt>zhī</rt></ruby><ruby>道<rt>dào</rt></ruby>)

4. **Chinese speakers learning English**: *I think he is right* (okay) vs *I think that he is right* (formal)
